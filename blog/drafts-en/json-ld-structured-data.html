JSON-LD Structured Data Guide: Help Google Understand Your Website

Travis Chen 2026-05-10 8 min read

Have you ever noticed special-looking search results on Google? Some display star ratings, others expand into FAQ sections, and some show recipe cooking times. Google doesn't generate these on its own — websites proactively provide this information through structured data.

And JSON-LD is the structured data format that Google officially recommends.

1. What Is JSON-LD?

JSON-LD stands for JavaScript Object Notation for Linked Data. Simply put, it's a block of JSON-formatted data placed inside an HTML <script> tag that tells search engines: "Here's what this page is about, who wrote it, when it was published, and what category it belongs to."

Compared to other structured data formats (like Microdata and RDFa), JSON-LD's biggest advantage is: it doesn't require any changes to your HTML content. Just add a <script type="application/ld+json"> block in the <head>, and search engines can understand your page.

This is why Google has explicitly stated: "We recommend using JSON-LD."

2. The Four Most Practical Schema Types

Schema.org defines hundreds of structured data types, but for most business websites, these four are the most useful:

1. Organization — Tell Google Who You Are

This is the most fundamental structured data. Place it on your homepage to tell Google your company name, logo, contact information, and social media links.

When someone searches for your company name, Google's Knowledge Panel may display this information.

2. LocalBusiness — Essential for Physical Locations

If you have a physical storefront or service location, LocalBusiness structured data tells Google your address, business hours, phone number, and service area.

This is especially helpful for local searches like "restaurants nearby" or "web design in [city]." Google Maps and local search results both reference LocalBusiness structured data.

3. Article — Get Your Content Properly Understood

Suitable for blogs, news, and tutorial articles. Article structured data includes the title, author, publication date, category, word count, and more.

In the HEY!BOSS blog, every article includes complete Article JSON-LD so Google can accurately understand the topic and author. The article you're reading right now has it.

4. FAQPage — Claim More Search Result Real Estate

FAQPage is the structured data type that most immediately boosts search result visibility. When Google recognizes your FAQPage structured data, search results expand to show Q&A content directly, taking up more screen space.

Every HEY!BOSS article includes 3 sets of FAQPage structured data, giving our articles higher click-through rates in search results.

3. JSON-LD Implementation Example

Here's an example of Article structured data:

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "Article",
  "headline": "Your Article Title",
  "description": "Article summary description",
  "datePublished": "2026-05-10",
  "author": {
    "@type": "Person",
    "name": "Author Name"
  },
  "publisher": {
    "@type": "Organization",
    "name": "Company Name"
  }
}
</script>

It's that simple. Just place this JSON inside the <head> of your HTML and you're done.

5. Common Mistakes and Best Practices

While JSON-LD syntax is simple, there are several common mistakes to avoid in implementation:

  • JSON format errors — A missing comma or extra bracket invalidates the entire structured data block. Use Google's Rich Results Test tool to validate
  • Content mismatch — If the title in your JSON-LD differs from the actual page title, Google may ignore your structured data
  • Fabricated information — Fake star ratings or non-existent FAQs in JSON-LD will result in Google penalizing your website
  • Missing required fields — Each Schema type has required fields; missing them means your data won't be recognized

  4. Validate with Rich Results Test — Confirm all structured data is correctly parsed
